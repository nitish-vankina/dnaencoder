Part 1
How text becomes DNA
Every piece of text on a computer is stored as numbers. The letter H is 72. The letter i is 105. These numbers are called bytes, and each byte is made of 8 binary digits — just 0s and 1s.

DNA has four bases: A, C, G, and T. Each base can represent exactly 2 bits. So if you split a byte into four 2-bit pairs, each pair maps to one base. That's the entire encoding — no tricks, no compression.
00AAdenine
01CCytosine
10GGuanine
11TThymine
One byte → 8 bits → four 2-bit groups → 4 bases. That's why the ratio is always 4. Every character you type expands to exactly four DNA bases.

Part 2
What is GC content and why does it matter?
In DNA, bases always pair up across the two strands: A pairs with T, and C pairs with G.

A–T pairs are held together by 2 hydrogen bonds.
C–G pairs are held together by 3 hydrogen bonds.

More bonds = stronger = harder to pull apart. A strand that's mostly G and C is very stable. A strand that's mostly A and T is weak and tends to fall apart.

The ideal range for DNA synthesis is 40–60% GC. Too low and the strand is fragile. Too high and it becomes rigid and difficult to work with chemically.

Part 3
Why does DNA storage matter?
The world generates an enormous amount of data — and we're running out of good ways to store it long term. Hard drives fail after a few years. Magnetic tape lasts maybe 30. Flash memory is measured in years, not decades.

Researchers at Microsoft, Harvard's Wyss Institute, and the European Bioinformatics Institute have all demonstrated working DNA storage systems. The encoding layer — exactly what this tool implements — is the solved part. The hard parts are synthesis speed and cost.

The space angle: on long-duration missions like a Mars trip or a Europa orbiter, data needs to survive for years without maintenance. Traditional storage degrades, especially under radiation. DNA in a protective medium could outlast any electronic storage we have — if we can protect it from the radiation that tries to corrupt it, which is exactly what the radiation tab models.
Part 4
How radiation corrupts DNA data
Ionizing radiation — the kind you get in space — carries enough energy to physically damage molecules. When it hits a DNA strand, a few things can happen:

Base damage: a high-energy particle knocks electrons out of a nucleotide, chemically altering it. An A might become something that reads as C. This is a bit flip at the molecular level — exactly like what happens to computer memory in space, except it's chemistry instead of electronics.

Strand break: the particle hits the backbone of the DNA helix and snaps it. A section of the strand becomes unreadable. In the simulation, these show as X.

The reason Europa is so extreme is that Jupiter has a massive magnetic field that traps charged particles in radiation belts around the planet. Europa orbits inside those belts. The radiation dose there is roughly 540 Sv per day — enough to kill a human in seconds and destroy unprotected electronics quickly. DNA storage there would need heavy error correction and redundancy just to survive.

How it works
Each byte is XOR'd with a mask value before encoding. Since XOR is its own inverse, applying the same mask again recovers the original. The optimizer tries all 256 possible mask values and picks whichever brings GC content closest to 50%. The mask is stored as the first 4 bases of the strand so the decoder knows how to reverse it.
